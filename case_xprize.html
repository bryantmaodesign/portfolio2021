X-PRIZE

Prototyping the Future of Personal Healthcare

Interaction Design, Industrial design

In 2012, Xprize launched the Qualcomm Tricorder XPRIZE, a $10 million global competition to incentivize innovation in personal healthcare. The mobile company HTC see the chance to use its technology to make health more accessible. The question is, how might we bring the bulky health devices and know-how into people's daily life?

Challenge

Today, very few methods exist for consumers to receive direct medical care without seeing a healthcare professional at a clinic or hospital, creating an access bottleneck. In 2012, the Xprize Foundation launched the competition 'Qualcomm Tricorder,' which aims to stimulate innovation of precision diagnostics that empower individuals to make informed health decisions in their daily lives.

As a Taiwan electronics company, HTC sees the opportunity to combine its core technology and the local medical and R&D resources to tackle the challenge while unlocking growth in the new market. In this project, I lead a UX team to collaborate with people across disciplines. To research, design, and build the system from strategy to the prototype's launch.

Approach

Using limitation as a drive for innovation

The message of the challenge is clear: to accurately capture 13 conditions and five real-time vital signs in the 72hrs test period via a system that is no more than 5 pounds. What's unclear is the complexity of the challenge, so we kickstart our exploration into three areas: the user's needs, the disease know-how, and technology possibility.

By collaborating with physicians and research labs, we build prototypes in different fidelity to probe user scenarios, uncover new diagnostic methods and validate test accuracy. The limitation forces the team to think out of the box and bring unconventional solutions through the iterative cycles. The result is an integrated diagnostic system that blends digital and physical touchpoints, with proof of delightfulness and effectiveness from the real patients.

Research

Sympathizing user scenarios and needs

Since anyone can use the system without healthcare workers' help, we need to explore different scenarios regardless of their health condition and technology familiarity. With limited access to our users, we interview people with a similar profile, build persona and user journey based on the guidelines. As the insights unfold, a few needs is clear for the team to focus on.

Fast and straightforward: A user might feel severe pain without technical know-how. The test flow must be simple enough for them to process quickly.

Compact and Comfortable: A user might bring the system with them all the time during the 72hrs test period. The system must be packed and comfortable wearing.

Skip Anytime: A user might be doing any activities (ex. cooking, working, traveling) while conducting the test. The diagnostic process should be able to pause at any given time.

Research

Learning diagnostic knowhow from the best practice

To build a robust diagnostic system, we partner with two top medical centers in Taiwan (NTUH and CGH) to deep dive into each target disease. We interview doctors to learn about the symptoms, cause, and treatment while observing how they applied the diagnostic skills if conditions permitted.

To quickly identify the potential conditions and avoid unnecessary tests, we also work with Dr. Andrew from Massachusetts General Hospital to develop a quick screening method inspired by the triage approach used in the emergency room.

Research

Explore emerging technology with research labs

The 5-pound limitation required very different technology from the conventional one. So we collaborate with the leading research labs (NTU, NCU, NTHU, NCKU) in Taiwan to explore and collect cutting-edge technology from Lab-on-a-chip devices to data processing algorithms to reduce the size of the final solution massively. We then evaluate the trade-offs of each technology with engineers, build prototypes to study the impacts on experience and effectiveness, and finally identify a set of candidate as the building blocks of the system.

Design

Defining the diagnose strategy and approach

With a holistic understanding of the user, disease, and technology, we are ready to build the system from the ground up. To ensure we don't work in silos, we first identify a high-level diagnostic flow from symptom screening to diagnose reports. We then discussed what medium is suitable to run each diagnosis.

Considering mobility and usability, we land on a mobile app with four gadgets equipped with biosensors (Vital sign, Fluidic, Image, and Breath). The app will be a central agent to help the user learn the gadgets and walk them through the diagnostic journey.

Design

Explore the gadget: do what the mobile can’t do

The 5-pound limitation and development cost force us to minimize the gadgets with only essential parts that mobile devices can not replace, such as high-resolution cameras and electrical probes. With the engineer's help, we build prototypes to explore and validate the form factor and tech feasibility and continue the iteration until a solution emerged that balancing usability, feasibility, and aesthetics.

Design

Mapped out the app flows and structure

As the gadget feature stablized, we host a 3-day workshop to map out all the mini flows and interactions for the 13 conditions diagnostic and five real-time vital signs monitoring. Considering a non-tech savvy user needs to complete a considerable amount of tasks in a given period. The team strives to simplify the app structure by reducing layers and using a step-by-step model to minimize the navigation hurdle. We also develop a design language to ensure the experience is consistent across digital and physical.

Testing

Iterating toward a delightful and robust experience

Can the system really help people to monitor their health with a delightful experience and full accuracy? This is the question we keep asking ourselves throughout the design journey. To answer the question, we work with QAs, Doctors, and the Xprize crew, conducting tests in different stages from concept validation to usability testing.

Dogfooding

Along the development cycle, we keep sending out working prototypes to our colleagues and their families and invite them to play with the system and report if they have any problems or ideas along the journey. The simple act helps us uncover many bugs and edge cases, such as battery burnout and connection problems, enabling the system to be more stable and comprehensive.

IRB Test

For disease using the new diagnosis method like shingles and COPD, we conduct IRB tests in the clinic by inviting patients to use the system to test their condition and compare results with the conventional one. While the test goal was primarily focused on diagnosis accuracy, we also discovered several pain points and opportunities to further improve the experience by observing from the side.

Testing

Remote User Testing

Before submission, the Xprize Foundation offers every team a chance to test their system and get feedback from the actual user. There are 5 participants in this test who are U.S citizens, mixed demographic and have one or more of the 13 target conditions (including the absence of disease). We observe the test session remotely and ask follow-up questions. We also study the system logs and experience scoresheet to spot the area of improvement for the final iteration.​

Outcome

The final system’s submission wins the 2nd place of Xprize from 300+ teams worldwide, with high scores on both user experience and diagnostic accuracy. Several breakthroughs of the test method also lead to patents in both medical and engineering. The accumulated know-how also set a foundation for the team to build other healthcare products, such as drug reminder and cancer patient management.

Final design

After 18 months of endeavor, we finally launched the prototype. An all-in-one diagnostic system under 5 pounds can capture 13 conditions and five vital signs in real-time. It consists of a mobile app and four distinct gadgets, each responsible for covering different diseases. For each disease, we crafted an interactive test flow based on the method and requirement. All the elements were tight into a neat, future-looking health kit, which aims to attract people to rethink personal health by helping them stay informed and make sensible decisions on their health.

01

All-in-one health kits

Build for portability

One primary cause that stops people from accessing healthcare is that the health devices are simply too bulky to move around. We put all our efforts into shrinking and integrating the diagnose devices, allowing any health worker or patient to bring it to the anyplace to run the diagnostic without losing accuracy.

Comfort and Discreet

No one wants to seem stupid, even when conducting a medical test. So we made the gadgets as discreet and comfortable as possible and reduced the required time and frequency. The non-intrusive looking also encourage people to try or even proud to wear it.

02

Fool-proof interaction

Speedy triage

No one wants to go through all 13 tests to identify their conditions. so we design a screening questionnaire based on the ER triage strategy. By analyzing the reported symptom set’s risk factors, a user is expected to do less than three diagnostic tests to identify their conditions by answering a few inquiries.

Personalize test plan

Without the health knowledge of each condition, it is difficult for the user to set up the test plan without worries what might goes wrong. We build the homepage as an agent to help them navigate through the test based on their conditions and lifestyles. The system will notify when to conduct which test, while keeping the user informed of their test results.

Step-by-step guidance

Considering the novelty and various user types, we strive to make the system as fool-proof as possible. We broke each diagnostic flow into five sections: 1) Intro, 2) Tutorial, 3) Setup 4) Test 5) Result and education. The step-by-step guidance helps the user focus on one thing at a time, finish the test smoothly even without any medical or technical know-how.

03

Innovative Test method

Shingles

Traditionally, Shingles required a doctor to diagnose based on the history of pain distribution, along with the telltale rash and blisters. We build a more straightforward diagnostic process combining a questionnaire and a self-report drawing task, which a user can finish the test in just in 3mins with 85% accuracy.

Urine collection

Anyone who has done a urine test in the hospital knows it's a terrible experience - dirty, embarrassed, and took a long wait time. Not to mention the partitioner needs to dip the test paper into someone else’s urine, which is not ideal. We create a urine stick that allows the user to pee directly on the test paper, cap it, send for a test, and get the result in 5 mins, with similar accuracy.
